Project overview

The product

Learny is a tutor marketplace app which aims to connect students and tutors easier and faster. Learny’s more focused on helping the beginner teachers who struggle to be noticed by students due to lack of experience, ratings, and reviews. For that matter there is a new feature introduced which randomly connects students and tutors for cheap trial lessons

Problem

There are several tutor marketplaces already existing but the problem for new tutors is to get noticed there. Students usually prefer more experienced tutors with good ratings, so beginner tutors are not likely to get a job soon.

Goal

Design an application which will connect beginner tutors with students easier and faster.

User Research

I conducted a series of interviews with 5 participants who are tutors or had tutoring experience in the past. After the interviews I was able to create personas which clearly showed the difficulties of getting students, especially in the beginning of the career as a tutor. For some participants the challenge of finding students was the reason of giving up on tutoring whatsoever. Also the feedback received through research made it clear that tutors would be willing to give trial lessons to get promoted.

Pain Points

1

High competition

New tutors struggle to compete with more experienced tutors with ratings

2

Time-consuming

It may take a lot of time to find students on tutor's marketplaces

3

Unstable income

Tutoring is an unstable job, so it is challenging to do it full-time and make a sustainable income out of it.

Usability study: findings

I conducted two usability studies on low-fidelity and high-fidelity prototypes. These were the main findings:

1

Statuses

Users need more obvious differentiation between statuses of the classes

2

Activity/Notifications

People had difficulty finding a screen with students’ requests for private classes

3

Group enrollments

Users would like better cues on how to keep track of students enrolling to group classes.

4

Minimum number of students

Users need better cues on how to understand the meaning of minimum number of students and how it affects the status of the class

Refining the design

Mockups

Based on the insights identified through the usability studies, there were some changes applied to the design

First Usability Study

Problem № 1

Users were confused with statuses of the classes

Solution

So I decided to add more statuses to make them more intuitive and easy to understand

Problem №2

Users struggled to find a screen with students' requests. It showed the importance of making notifications more clear

Solution

I moved notification to the tabbar, so users wouldn’t struggle to find it

Second Usability Study

Problem №1

Second usability study showed that users struggled to keep track of group classes filling up

Solution №1

In addition to indicating number of students enrolled to the class, I added a screen with a list of students in the group

Problem №2

Users were confused about the concept of minimum number of students in group classes

Solution

I added more visual and text cues on the concept of minimum number of students required to activate the group class

Clear labels for interactive elements that can be read by screen readers

Takeaways

Impact:

The idea of randomly connecting tutors to students was appealing to participants, they saw it as a good opportunity to start the tutoring career and gain more students.

One quote from a participant clearly indicates this idea:

“It’s a great place to gain more experience and confidence as a teacher”

What I learned:

I learned that even a small feature, as randomly connecting students to tutors, consists of a complex process in order to make it easy to understand and easy to use. Also I understood that less doesn’t always mean better and simpler. Sometimes it’s optimal to use more words in order to deliver message in a clearer way.
